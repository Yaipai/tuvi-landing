# How Tuvi uses artificial intelligence

> This page exists because we owe you transparency. If the AI proposes, you should know what it proposes, how, and what to do when it gets things wrong.

## What do we generate with AI?

Tuvi uses generative models to produce:

- Full curriculum-aligned learning situations (objectives, sequence, assessment).
- Exams with marking schemes (questions, criteria and answers).
- Cooperative activities, handouts, summaries and glossaries.
- Visual resources: charts, maps, diagrams, chemical structures, music scores.
- Pupil tracking notes built from the notes you write yourself.

## How are they generated?

- **Advanced third-party models.** We use the best models available at any given time, and we rotate them whenever better alternatives appear. The edge isn't the model — it's what we do with it.
- **The curriculum as anchor.** The model does not invent the curriculum. Tuvi feeds it the official syllabus you've chosen (LOMLOE, National Curriculum + A-Level, ACARA, IB, Cambridge) so every output is anchored to a concrete block and competence.
- **Automated validators.** For topics with verifiable facts (dates, formulas, literary quotations) Tuvi runs a second "fact-checker" model before handing the material back to you. If something looks doubtful, we flag it inside the material.
- **The teacher decides.** Every material is handed over for you to review before taking it into the classroom. The AI proposes; it does not impose.

## What can go wrong?

We'll say this plainly:

- **Historical dates and milestones.** The AI can be wrong about years or attributions. Check any date before printing.
- **Literary or philosophical quotations.** They may not be word-perfect. If you're going to use them in class, cross-check against the physical edition.
- **Numerical work.** In maths, science or economics exercises, validate the solution before marking it as correct.
- **Generated images.** In diagrams and charts the AI can over-simplify. If an image doesn't represent the concept well, ask for a new one or use an alternative.
- **Cultural and linguistic bias.** The models are trained mostly on English-language and US-centric text. If something doesn't fit your context (for example, overly American examples in a literature class), edit it.

## What do we do to minimise errors?

- Anchoring to the official curriculum at every generation.
- Structural validators: that an exam has an answer section, that a learning situation covers the block's objectives, that a diagram has the parts it should have.
- Automated tests over thousands of outputs before any change reaches production.
- Explicit notice on every material: "Generated with AI. Review it before taking it to class."

## Why don't you say which model you use?

Because the edge isn't the model — it's the teaching specialisation. How we anchor to the curriculum, what validators run, how we write the prompts for your context. Models change every few months; Tuvi picks the best one for each task (one for text, another for image, another for marking) and rotates them whenever better alternatives appear. Naming this month's vendor adds nothing for you. What does add value is knowing that you remain the pedagogical authority.

## What data does the AI use?

What you give it and nothing more:

- The curriculum context you've chosen (subject, year group, block).
- Your teaching preferences (methodology, duration, language).
- Your classroom notes, if you choose to use them to generate reports.
- We do **not** use your data to train models.
- We do **not** share your data with third parties for advertising.
